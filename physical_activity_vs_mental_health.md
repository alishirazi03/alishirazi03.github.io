## Slide 2: Research Question

### Does Exercise Actually Help Mental Health?

**The Central Question:**
> Is there a measurable, statistical relationship between physical activity levels and mental health outcomes in students?

**Why This Matters:**
- Mental health crisis among students at all-time high
- Stress, anxiety, depression rates increasing
- Need evidence-based interventions

---

## Slide 3: Background & Context

### The Modern Student Mental Health Crisis

**Unique Challenges Our Generation Faces:**
- 📱 Constant social media exposure
- 📚 Intense academic pressure
- 💻 Sedentary, screen-based lifestyle
- 🏃 Reduced outdoor physical activity

**The Shift:**
- Previous generations: More physically active, more outdoor time

## Slide 4: Why Physical Activity?

### The Hypothesis

**Biological Mechanisms:**
- Release of endorphins (natural mood enhancers)

- Improved sleep quality
- Enhanced brain function

**Social & Psychological Benefits:**
- Social interaction through group activities
- Sense of accomplishment

## Slide 5: Study Aims & Objectives

### Research Goals

**Primary Aim:**
Examine the statistical relationship between physical activity and mental health outcomes (stress, anxiety, depression) among students.

**Specific Objectives:**
1. **Measure** physical activity levels (frequency, duration, intensity)
2. **Assess** mental health status using validated clinical tools
3. **Analyze** statistical relationships and correlations
4. **Identify** mechanisms linking activity to mental health
5. **Inform** evidence-based intervention design

## Slide 6: Methodology - Data Collection

### How We Measured Variables

**Physical Activity Assessment:**
- Standardized questionnaires
- Frequency, duration, and intensity metrics
- Self-reported activity logs
- Wearable activity monitors (where available)

**Mental Health Assessment - Validated Clinical Tools:**
- **GAD-7:** Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale
- **PHQ-9:** Patient Health Questionnaire (depression)
- **PSS:** Perceived Stress Scale

**Why These Tools?**
- Scientifically validated
- Used in clinical practice
- Standardized scoring
- Reliable and reproducible

## Slide 7: Statistical Methods

### Tools for Analysis

**1. Correlation Analysis**
- **Pearson Correlation:** Measures linear relationship strength
- **Spearman's Rank:** For non-normal data distributions
- **Purpose:** Determine if variables move together

**2. Regression Analysis**
- **Linear Regression:** Predict mental health from activity levels
- **Multiple Regression:** Control for confounding variables
- **Purpose:** Understand cause-effect relationships

**3. Comparative Analysis**
- **Independent t-tests:** Compare two groups (active vs. inactive)
- **ANOVA:** Compare multiple groups (low, moderate, high activity)
- **Purpose:** Identify significant group differences

## Slide 9: Results - Key Findings

### Strong Positive Correlation Identified

**Statistical Results:**
- **Correlation coefficient (r):** 0.68
- **Interpretation:** Strong positive relationship
- **p-value:** < 0.05 (statistically significant)
- **Effect size:** ~46% of variance explained by physical activity

**What This Means:**
Students who exercise more have measurably better mental health outcomes.

**This is NOT random chance.**

---

## Slide 10: Results - Stress Scores

### Impact on Stress Levels

![Graph showing stress scores decreasing with physical activity levels]

**Key Finding:**
Students with **high physical activity** reported stress scores **35-40% LOWER** than sedentary peers.

**Activity Level → Average Stress Score:**
- Low Activity: 7.2/10
- Moderate Activity: 5.8/10
- High Activity: 4.3/10

**Statistical Significance:** p < 0.001

## Slide 11: Results - Anxiety Scores

### Impact on Anxiety Levels

![Graph showing anxiety scores decreasing with physical activity levels]

**Parallel Pattern to Stress:**
- Similar dose-response relationship
- Higher activity = Lower anxiety
- Progressive improvement across activity levels

**GAD-7 Scores by Activity Level:**
- Low Activity: High anxiety symptoms

- High Activity: Minimal symptoms

## Slide 12: Dose-Response Relationship

### How Much Exercise Matters

**Critical Finding:**
It's not just IF you exercise, but HOW MUCH.

**Dose-Response Pattern:**
```
More Exercise → Better Mental Health
(Linear relationship with threshold effects)
```

**Optimal Activity Levels:**
- Minimum: 2-3 sessions/week shows benefits
- Moderate: 3-4 sessions/week significant improvement
- High: 5+ sessions/week maximum benefit

**Intensity Also Matters:**
Moderate-to-vigorous activity > light activity

---

## Slide 13: Mechanisms Identified

### WHY Does Exercise Help?

**Three Primary Mechanisms:**

**1. Improved Sleep Quality (r = 0.54)**
- Regular exercise → Better sleep patterns
- Better sleep → Lower stress/anxiety
- Mediating variable in regression model

**2. Enhanced Social Support (r = 0.48)**
- Group activities → Social connections
- Team sports → Reduced isolation
- Social interaction → Mental well-being

**3. Increased Self-Esteem (r = 0.61)**
- Fitness goals achieved → Confidence boost
- Physical improvement → Self-image improvement
- Accomplishment → Reduced depression

---

## Slide 14: Regression Analysis Results

### Controlling for Confounding Variables

**Multiple Regression Model:**

**Independent Variables:**
- Physical activity level (primary)
- Sleep quality
- Social support frequency
- Baseline mental health

**Dependent Variable:** Mental health score

**Results:**
- Physical activity: β = -0.52 (p < 0.001)
- Sleep quality: β = -0.31 (p < 0.01)
- Social support: β = -0.24 (p < 0.05)

**Interpretation:**
Physical activity remains strongest predictor even when controlling for other factors.

---

## Slide 15: Group Comparisons (ANOVA)

### Comparing Activity Levels

**ANOVA Results:**
F(2, 297) = 42.8, p < 0.001

**Post-hoc Analysis (Tukey HSD):**
- Low vs. Moderate: p < 0.01 ✓
- Moderate vs. High: p < 0.05 ✓
- Low vs. High: p < 0.001 ✓✓✓

**Conclusion:**
Significant differences exist between ALL activity level groups.

## Slide 17: Limitations

### Study Constraints & Considerations

**1. Causation vs. Correlation**
- Cross-sectional design limits causal inference
- Could be: Exercise → Mental health
- Or: Better mental health → More exercise
- **Future:** Longitudinal study needed

**2. Sample Demographics**
- Primarily university students
- Geographic limitation
- **Generalizability:** Need diverse samples

**3. Optimal Dose Unknown**
- Found relationship, not exact prescription
- Individual variation likely
- **Future:** RCTs to determine optimal levels

**4. Self-Report Bias**
- Activity levels self-reported
- Mental health self-assessed
- **Mitigation:** Used validated instruments
